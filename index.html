What is the Waste Mapping Phase?

Establishing the feedstock baseline for the PhD

Before materials can be developed, the feedstock must be quantified.

This phase focuses on identifying, quantifying, and spatially mapping agricultural and agro-industrial waste streams in Lebanon. It serves as the scientific foundation for the entire PhD project, ensuring that subsequent material development is based on resources that are available, abundant, and accessible.

  • Transitions from the MCDA Regional Selection (Lebanon) to actionable local data.
  • Prioritizes wastes suitable for bio-based construction materials.
  • Addresses the critical data gap in Lebanon's waste management infrastructure.

Methodology Note: National waste totals come from credible studies. For regions where specific data exists, those values are used directly. For regions without official measurements, the numbers are estimated using local production patterns and typical waste generated per crop (residue-to-product ratios). These regional figures give a good indication of scale but should be viewed as ranges rather than precise audited values.

‚ö†Ô∏è Current Limitations

  • Missing Direct Measurements: Reliance on standard ratios rather than direct weighing in Lebanese fields.
  • Regional Uncertainty: Split based on production data, not waste audit data.
  • Lack of Field Surveys: Moisture content and contamination levels are theoretical.

üöÄ Phase 2 Actions

  • 1 Prioritisation (MCDA): Select top 3-5 priority waste streams identified during the mapping phase.
  • 2 Field Visits & Networking: Establish relationships with stream sources/suppliers.
  • 3 Sampling Campaign: Collect physical samples from different regions/sources.
  • 4 Preliminary Characterisation: Local/On-site testing (Acquisition Phase).
  • 5 In-Depth Lab Analysis: Full characterisation at University of Bath.
  • 6 Experimental Planning: Job sheet creation & mix design planning.

Where This Phase Leaves Us

The waste mapping dashboard serves as the quantitative baseline. It confirms that Lebanon possesses sufficient, diverse, and accessible lignocellulosic waste to support a bio-based materials industry, validating the previous MCDA regional selection.
